Gödels Theorem (2019)

Remark: This is a summary of specific parts from "Goedel, Escher, Bach" by Douglas Hofstaedter and aims to offer a comprehensive guide towards Goedels proof. All contents are directly extracted from the book and I do not claim ownership over anything. All contents of this page are summarizations of "Goedel, Escher, Bach".
On this page Systems of Symbols which operate by typographical rules are examined. A very well known example of such a system is mathematical logic. In the following, a List of rules for Propositional Calculus will be formulated. Futhermore, a short explanation of Gödel Numbering will be given on the example of a MIU-system. Afterwards we will get to the core of the matter by examining TNT - Typographical Number Theory and outlining the general direction of Goedels proof.



List of rules:

It is a strenght of (mathematical) logic to take a statement which is common-sense and pack it into formal and meaningless symbolic operations.
Rule 1) gives a good example for that. The trivial and logical statement becomes apparent when one knows that \( \land \) stands for "and" in mathematical notation. However, the interpreted meaning of the symbol is not necessarily in the fore-ground of a logical examination. Often it can be better to regard the system as purely typographical with a given set of manipulation rules and without interpreted meaning. A list of mathematical (typographical) rules is outlined in the following:

Theorem rules:
1) If \(x\) and \(y\) are theorems, then \(< x \land y >\) is a theorem.
2) If \(< x \land y >\) is a theorem, then both \(x\) and \(y\) are theorems.
3) The string '~~' can be deleted from any theorem. It can also be inserted into any theorem, provided that the result is still well-formed.
4) If \(y\) can be derived when \(x\) is assumed to be a theorem, then \(< x \subset y >\) is a theorem.
5) Inside a fantasy (4) any theorem from the "reality" one level higher can be brought in and used.
6) If \(x\) and \(< x \subset y >\) are both theorems, then \(y\) is a theorem.
7) \(< x \supset y >\) and \(< ~x \subset ~y >\) are interchangeable.
8) \(< ~x \land ~y >\) and \(~< x \lor y >\) are interchangeable.

Formation rules:
If x and y are well formed, then the following strings are also well formed:
1) \(~x\)
2)\(< x \land y >\)
3)\(< x \lor y >\)
3)\(< x \subset y >\)

Intented Interpretation of Symbols:
\( \land \) - and
~ - not
< ... > - groupers
\( \lor \) - or

It makes sense, when reading a theorem of Propositional Calculus to interpret anything but the atoms. For example the theorem \(< P \lor \text{~} P >\) is read as "P or not P". Despite the fact that P is not a sentence it is easy to see that the resulting sentence will be true, no matter what is chosen for P. The Power of Propositional Calculus is, that it gives a set of rules for producing statements which would be true in all conceivable worlds. Furthermore those statements can be produced by a set of typographical rules



Gödel Numbering explained by introducing the MIU system:

On the example of the MIU system Gödel Numbering is outlined. This is a method to convert typographical symbol manipulations into arithmetical operations. First, the basic Axioms and Rules of the MIU-system are explained, then Axiom: MI
Rules:
(1) If you posses a string whose last letter is I, you can add a U at the end.
(2) Suppose you have Mx. Then Mxx is also an allowed string
(3) You may replace III by U, however not U by III
(4) If UU occurs within a string you may drop it

From typographical to arithmetical:
M < - > 3
I < - > 1
U < - > 0
MU < - > 30
...
Derivation of strings:
(1) MI < - > 31
(2) MII < - > 311
(3) MIII < - > 3111
(4) MUI < - > 301
(5) MUIU < - > 3010
(6) MUIUUIU < - > 3010010
(7) MUIIU < - > 30100

The former typographical rules can now be transformed into arithmetical steps:

(1) \(10m +1\) can be made into \(10*(10m+1)\)
(2) \(3*10^{m}+n\) can be maade into \(10^{m}*(3*10^{m}+n)+n\)
(3) \(k'10^{m+3}+111*10^{m}+n\) can be made into \(k*10^{m+1}+n\)
(4) \(k*10^{m+2}+n\) can be makde into \(k*10^{m}+n \)
Axiom: 31

Proposition: If there is a typographical rule which tells us how certain digits are to be shifted, changed, dropped or inserted in any number represented decimally, then this rule can be represented equally well by an arithmetical counterpart which involves operations with powers of 10 as well as additions, subtractions and so forth.
< - >
Typographical rules for manipulating numbers are actually arithmetical rules for operating on numbers.
Now "Is Mu a theorem of the MIU-system?" is equivalent to "Is 30 a MIU-number?".
This sentence is translateable into TNT (typographical number theory) since it is a statement of number theory.



TNT - Typographical Number Theory:

Numerals:
zero: O
one: SO
two: SSO
three: SSSO
etc.

The 5 axioms:
(1) /( /)
(2) /( /)
(3) /( /)
(4) /( /)
(5) /( /)


Variables:
a b c d
a' b' c' d'
a'' b'' c'' d''
. . . .

Example: \( 1+2+3 -> (SO + (SSO + SSSO)) \)
All symbols of Propositional Calculus are adopted. Atoms of TNT are statements of equality. E.g. \( (SSO + SSS0) = SSSSO \)

Rules of Well-Formedness:
1) Numerals: \( O \) is a numeral. Any numeral preceded by \( S \)is also a numeral
2) Variables: a,b,c,d are variables. Any variable followed by ' is also a variable
3) Terms: \( \forall numerals \land variables \) are terms. Any term preceded by \( S \) is a term
4) Atoms: If S and t are terms, then so are \((s+t)\) and \((s*t)\)
5) Negations: a well-formed formula preceded by ~ is well formed
5) Compounds: If x and y are well formed and no free variable is quantified in both, then the following are well formed:
\( < x \land y > , < x \lor y > , < x \subset y >\)
6) Quantification: If \( u \) is a variable and \( x \) is well formed with the free variable u in it, then the following is well formed: \( \exists u:x \) and \( \forall u:x \)

Rule of Specification: Suppose the variable u occurs within a string \(x \). If \( \forall u : x \) is a theorem, then so is \(x \) and so are any strings made by replacing u
Rule of Generalization:: Suppose \(x \) is a theorem in which \( u \) is a free variable. The \( \forall u: x \) is a theorem
Rule of Interchange: Suppose \( u \) is a variable. Then the strings \( \forall u:~ \) and \( ~\exists u \) are interchangable
Rule of Existance: Suppose a term appears once of multiply in a theorem. Then any of the appearances my be replaced by a variable which otherwise does not occur in the theorem and the corresponding quantifier must be placed in front.
Rule of Equality: If \( r = s \) is a theorem then so is \( s = r \)
If \( r = s \) and \(s=t \)are theorems, then \( r = t \) is a theorem
Rule of Successorship: If \( r = t \) is a theorem, then \( Sr = St \) is a theorem
If \( Sr = St \) is a theorem, then \( r = t \) is a theorem
Rule of Induction: If \( u \) is a variable and \( X(u) \) is well formed, then theoremhood of \( \forall u: < X(u) \subset X(Su|u) > \) and \( X(O|u) \) asserts \( \forall u: X(u) \)
Incompleteness and Inconsistency: Using the specified rules one can produce
\( (O + O) = O \)
\( (O + SO) = SO \)
\( (O + SSO) = SSO \)
etc.

However the summarizing string of this series \( \forall a: (O + a) = a \) is not producable.
This kind of incompleteness is called \( \omega \)-Incompleteness.
Additionally the string \( ~\forall a : (O+a)=a \) is also not a theorem. Neither of those is decideable within the system.
Adding \( ~\forall a : (O+a) = a \) seems to create am inconsistency between the pyramidal string family which asserts that all numbers have the states property and the statement that not all numbers have it. However imagining some unsuspected supernatural numbers solves this.
This kind of inconsistency is calles \( \omega \)-inconsistency.
